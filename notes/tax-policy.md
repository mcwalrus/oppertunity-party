Here's how I actually consume a site, and what that implies, roughly in priority order.

**1. Plain, server-rendered HTML at stable public URLs — this is 90% of it.** When I fetch a page, I read the HTML the server returns and extract the text. Your `/tax-reset` page is exactly right: real text, proper headings, a clean FAQ. Anything that renders only via JavaScript, sits behind a login, or lives inside a document viewer is where I struggle. If the canonical version of a policy is a normal web page, I (and every other AI tool, plus search crawlers) can read it reliably. This is the single highest-leverage thing and it's free.

**2. If you keep PDFs, self-host them at a direct URL.** The exact problem we hit: a `drive.google.com/file/d/.../view` link returns the viewer shell, not the file. If instead you host it at something like `opportunity.org.nz/downloads/tax-reset.pdf`, I can fetch it and extract the text directly — I have PDF text extraction, it just needs an actual PDF at the end of the URL, not a Drive viewer. One-line fix for the thing that failed today.

**3. Semantic HTML.** Real headings (h1–h3), lists, and live text rather than text baked into images. Your FAQ structure is already ideal for this. Avoid putting key numbers (costings, tax rates) only inside infographic images — put them in text too.

**4. Schema.org structured data via JSON-LD.** This is the genuinely *accepted* machine-readable standard — a `<script type="application/ld+json">` block embedded in the page, the same thing search engines have used for years. There's a `FAQPage` type that maps directly onto the FAQ you already have, and `Article`/`WebPage` for the policy itself. It makes your structure unambiguous to any parser. Well-established, widely supported, worth doing.

**5. Crawl plumbing: `robots.txt` + `sitemap.xml`.** A sitemap lists all your policy URLs so they're discoverable; robots.txt is also where you'd make a *deliberate* choice about which AI crawlers you allow (ClaudeBot, GPTBot, PerplexityBot, etc.) — worth a conscious decision for a political party, since you may want maximum reach or may want some control.

**6. `llms.txt` — the emerging convention aimed exactly at your question, but be realistic.** It's a markdown file at your domain root giving AI systems a curated map of your key pages. Honest current state, as of mid-2026: it's a community convention rather than a formal standard, maintained through the llmstxt.org project, with no IETF standardization, sitting at roughly a 10% adoption rate across a 300,000-domain study. Crucially for your use case, the major AI crawlers — GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot and others — overwhelmingly skip the file and crawl HTML directly. So it's cheap insurance for the future and a nice clean brand summary, but a low-cost, low-yield bet today — not a substitute for points 1–4.

One nuance worth naming: there are two different scenarios. One is a *background crawler* indexing your whole site (where sitemap/robots/llms.txt matter). For that second flow, which is probably how voters and journalists will actually use tools like me, only one thing matters: is the content at a fetchable URL in readable form? Clean HTML pages and directly-hosted PDFs nail that; Drive links and JS-only pages break it.
